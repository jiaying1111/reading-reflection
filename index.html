Reading Reflection

Week 1

Laurel Schwulst’s “My website is a shifting house next to a river of knowledge. What could yours be?

Reading Laurel Schwulst’s writing helped me rethink what a website can be and why I might want to make one. Instead of seeing a website as a finished portfolio or a place to “explain myself clearly,” I started to understand it as a space that can be personal, unfinished, and alive. Schwulst’s idea that a website can be a room, a garden, or even a puddle made me feel less pressure to make something perfect, and more freedom to make something honest. Inspired by this, my first website would not be organized by time or productivity, but by language and feeling. nstead of telling one clear story from beginning to end, the site presents fragments of a narrative—poems, short texts, or scenes—that can be entered from many points. Rather than presenting a clear narrative, the site would allow ambiguity and softness. It could be something you wander through, rather than something you “understand” immediately. This idea connects to Project 1 because it treats the website itself as the work, not just a container for other works. The structure, typography, spacing, and navigation all become part of the meaning. Like Schwulst describes, the website would shape how I see myself during this transitional moment, while also changing as I add to it over time. Overall, this reading encouraged me to see my first website as a space for exploration rather than resolution—a place to sit with uncertainty, process change, and let ideas grow slowly.

Week 2

J. R. Carpenter, A Handmade Web

Reading J. R. Carpenter’s writing on the “handmade web” made me more aware of how different the early web was from the one we experience today, and how much has been lost along the way. The idea of the handmade web emphasizes slowness, individuality, and care—qualities that feel increasingly rare in a digital environment dominated by platforms, templates, and algorithms. Carpenter’s description of hand-coded websites as provisional, personal, and sometimes temporary helped me see these qualities not as limitations, but as strengths. What stood out to me most was the comparison between handmade web pages and zines or artist books. Like those physical formats, handmade websites can be intimate, experimental, and unconcerned with polish or scale. The fact that many early web works still exist in their original medium, even as technologies around them change, highlights how the web can function as a living archive rather than a static record. This reading encouraged me to rethink my own approach to web-making. Instead of aiming for efficiency, visibility, or completeness, it made me interested in building websites that embrace imperfection, ephemerality, and personal authorship.

Week 3

Hello World by Taeyoon Choi

Reading Hell World by Taeyoon Choi made me rethink my relationship with computers—not as seamless tools, but as layered objects shaped by labor, history, and abstraction. What stood out to me most was Choi’s decision to physically build a computer in order to understand it. This act resists the “black box” nature of contemporary technology, where interfaces are designed to be smooth while concealing their inner logic. By slowing down and engaging with circuits, repetition, and failure, Choi reframes computation as something human, imperfect, and poetic. I was especially drawn to the idea that computation, architecture, and art share a common language of abstraction and repetition. The comparison between microchips, abstract paintings, and city plans helped me see how systems scale—from silicon to cities—while still being shaped by human values and decisions. At the same time, Choi reminds us that life cannot be reduced to binary logic. Cities, like people, are unpredictable and full of glitches. This reading connects strongly to ideas of handmade or experimental approaches to technology. It suggests that understanding how things are built gives us agency to question what kinds of systems we want to live with.
